In Loving Rememberance

Born in 1849, Fort Worth first drew breath as a frontier outpost along the Trinity River — one of a line of forts defending the edge of the Texas frontier. In its youth, it was rough and loud, known as “Hell's Half Acre,” a rowdy refuge for drifters, soldiers, and cowboys pushing cattle north. But over time, this wild child grew into something greater: a rail hub, an oil boomtown, a beacon of art and architecture, and finally, the proud “City of Cowboys and Culture.”

Its life story is etched in its landmarks: the peaceful Trinity Duck Pond, where the city finds reflection; the steadfast First United Methodist Church, a symbol of faith that weathered the Great Depression; the once-mighty T&P Warehouse, now fallen silent — one of our dearly departed; the JFK Tribute, a solemn reminder of promise interrupted; and the historic Fort Worth Power Plant, whose rusting bones still hum faintly of progress long gone, another of the deceased.

Yet Fort Worth endures. Through every rise and fall, its spirit persists — equal parts grit and grace, memory and reinvention — living proof that even the ghosts of this city still have stories to tell.
